Market thesis

Agent memory is a cache. Personal context is the database of record.

Memory providers assume continuity belongs to an agent. Fulcra’s view is more durable: continuity belongs to the person, the household, the body, the possessions, the environment, and the life timeline. Agents come and go. The personal context layer persists.

25–300 GBRaw context per year for an average present-day user.
1–30 TBRaw context per year for a present-day power user.
50–500+ TBPossible annual context exhaust for future high-bandwidth users.

The right product is not a memory store. It is a knowledge store for all the personal data that will never be written to the Internet.

The process is context gathering: always-on ingest plus deterministic ETL that produces organized time series, entities, documents, preferences, and provenance. Agents should call the Life API; they should not burn time and tokens rebuilding the ETL pipeline.

The three-layer sizing model

Storage explodes when Fulcra preserves raw artifacts. The agent-facing layer stays comparatively small if data is normalized and indexed correctly.

01 / RAW ARCHIVE

Originals and high-fidelity exhaust

PDFs, scans, photos, videos, device exports, camera/audio, vehicle logs, home-camera clips, medical imaging.

02 / NORMALIZED CONTEXT

Canonical, queryable life data

Time series, events, entities, transactions, preferences, OCR, embeddings, summaries, graph edges, provenance.

03 / LIFE API

Agent-usable endpoints

Cheap, permissioned calls that answer what changed, where the user was, what they prefer, and what matters now.

Three present-day user scenarios

The structured knowledge graph stays manageable. The raw archive becomes strategically important precisely because it is too large and too messy for every agent builder to reconstruct.

Average

Normal consumer

25–300 GB/yr

Mostly photos, videos, scanned mail, receipts, a few wearables, calendars, location, and maybe home-camera clips.

  • Normalized: 0.5–5 GB/yr
  • Dominant driver: camera roll
  • Life API: cheap and fast
Power

Quantified / connected user

1–30 TB/yr

Many sensors, heavy media, smart home, car telemetry, medical imaging, business or family document flows.

  • Normalized: 10–100+ GB/yr
  • Dominant driver: video + telemetry
  • Needs retention tiers
Extreme

Creator / many-camera household

30–200+ TB/yr

Continuous cameras, AV-like logs, rich media production, dense world capture, large household/estate graph.

  • Normalized: 100 GB–1 TB/yr
  • Dominant driver: raw media
  • Potentially PB-scale in future

High-value, low-byte context

The data that makes agents excellent is often tiny. Its value comes from being normalized, current, permissioned, and connected to provenance.

Preference graph

Seat, hotel, food, travel, shopping, accessibility, notification, privacy, and household preferences.

Possession graph

What the user owns, what breaks, what needs replacing, what is under warranty, what has compatible parts.

Place graph

Home, work, doctors, schools, favorite stores, gyms, recurring errands, avoided locations, commute patterns.

Medical constraints

Allergies, medications, care plans, clinicians, labs, insurance, diagnoses, and high-salience care instructions.

Personal admin graph

IDs, policies, taxes, bills, deadlines, applications, licenses, renewals, local-government interactions.

Taste graph

What the user watches, listens to, reads, buys, skips, saves, returns, rates, and recommends.

Recommended platform architecture

Do not build one monolithic memory store. Build a tiered context platform with hot facts, warm time series, graphs, vectors, raw archive, and policy.

Hot contextCurrent facts, active tasks, recent events, high-confidence preferences, summaries.
Warm time seriesHealth, location, transactions, smart home, calendar, media, app events.
Knowledge graphPeople, places, possessions, providers, accounts, devices, entities, relationships.
Semantic layerEmbeddings over docs, scans, images, clips, OCR, summaries, and multimodal extracts.
Cold archivePDFs, scans, photos, videos, exports, DICOM, car logs, raw sensor streams.
Policy layerConsent, scope, retention, deletion, correction, audit, and agent permissions.

Life API examples

Agents should call organized endpoints. They should not perform raw ingest, OCR, deduplication, schema inference, or compaction themselves.

GET /life/location/visits?range=2026-01-01..2026-05-29
GET /life/health/sleep?range=last_90_days
GET /life/purchases/items?merchant=Home%20Depot
GET /life/preferences/travel
GET /life/documents/search?q=warranty%20water%20heater
GET /life/possessions/warranties/expiring
GET /life/media/taste/profile
GET /life/context/briefing?task=book_trip

A memory provider stores what an agent learned. Fulcra stores what a person’s life produces.

For today’s users, that is tens to hundreds of GB per year of raw personal context, compressible into GB-scale normalized knowledge. For power users, it is already TB-scale. Once first-person cameras, richer car telemetry, medical imaging, and home sensors become normal, the context-gathering layer becomes tens to hundreds of TB per user per year unless aggressively summarized and tiered.

Notes. This briefing is an order-of-magnitude sizing model. Ranges separate raw retained artifacts from normalized agent-ready context. The omitted category is private messaging bodies such as email and text messages. Figures should be refined with source-specific exports, retention policy, compression assumptions, and Fulcra’s actual schema sizes.
